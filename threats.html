Threats

Common cyber threats to know

Modern attackers use a mix of deception and technical tricks to gain access to accounts, data, and systems.

📧

Phishing

Fraudulent emails or messages that imitate trusted brands to steal login credentials or money.

Explanation

Phishing relies on urgency, fear, or curiosity to make you click a link or open an attachment quickly.

Example

A fake invoice email asks you to confirm your account by clicking a link that steals your password.

Quick quiz

1. What is the safest response to a suspicious password-reset email?

  • A. Click the link immediately
  • B. Reply with your password
  • C. Open the official site manually and verify

2. Why do attackers use urgency in phishing messages?

  • A. To make you act quickly without thinking
  • B. To improve email delivery
  • C. To avoid spam filters

3. What should you do before clicking a link from an email?

  • A. Hover over it and verify the sender
  • B. Click it immediately to test it
  • C. Share it with coworkers

4. Which is a strong security habit?

  • A. Trusting every urgent request
  • B. Verifying requests through official channels
  • C. Reusing the same password

5. What is the main goal of phishing?

  • A. To improve your browser speed
  • B. To steal information or money
  • C. To clean your inbox

Answer: 1-C, 2-A, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B.

🦠

Malware

Malicious software, including spyware and trojans, designed to infect devices and collect data.

Explanation

Malware can silently install on a device and monitor activity, steal files, or disrupt normal operations.

Example

A fake software installer secretly adds spyware that records keystrokes and sends them to hackers.

Quick quiz

1. Which action is most likely to reduce malware risk?

  • A. Downloading random browser extensions
  • B. Installing software only from trusted sources
  • C. Disabling your antivirus forever

2. What type of malware records your keystrokes?

  • A. Adware
  • B. Spyware
  • C. Cloud backup

3. Why should you avoid cracked software?

  • A. It may contain hidden malware
  • B. It runs faster
  • C. It is always free

4. What helps keep malware away from your device?

  • A. Downloading from pop-ups
  • B. Keeping your software updated
  • C. Turning off security alerts

5. What is a common way malware spreads?

  • A. Through trusted websites only
  • B. Through malicious downloads and attachments
  • C. Through a strong password alone

Answer: 1-B, 2-B, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B.

🔒

Ransomware

Encryption malware that locks files until a ransom is paid, often causing severe disruption.

Explanation

Ransomware encrypts important documents and demands payment for their release, making recovery difficult.

Example

A company finds its shared folders encrypted after an employee opens a malicious attachment.

Quick quiz

1. Why should you keep regular backups?

  • A. So files are easier to lose
  • B. To recover files if ransomware strikes
  • C. To make your device slower

2. What does ransomware typically do to files?

  • A. It deletes them permanently
  • B. It encrypts them and demands payment
  • C. It saves them to the cloud

3. What is a good first response if you suspect ransomware?

  • A. Disconnect the affected device
  • B. Pay the ransom immediately
  • C. Open more attachments

4. Why are backups valuable?

  • A. They reduce the chance of infection
  • B. They help restore data without paying a ransom
  • C. They replace antivirus software

5. Which action increases ransomware risk?

  • A. Opening trusted links only
  • B. Clicking unknown attachments
  • C. Using MFA

Answer: 1-B, 2-B, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B.

🎭

Social Engineering

Manipulating people through trust, urgency, or authority to obtain sensitive information or access.

Explanation

Attackers often exploit human habits rather than technical weaknesses, using impersonation and pressure.

Example

Someone calls pretending to be IT support and asks you to share a one-time code from your phone.

Quick quiz

1. What is the best response to a caller asking for a verification code?

  • A. Share it to prove you are authorized
  • B. Hang up and verify the request through official channels
  • C. Give them the code and ask questions later

2. What is social engineering mainly about?

  • A. Improving software performance
  • B. Tricking people into revealing information
  • C. Creating stronger passwords

3. Why are attackers successful in social engineering?

  • A. They exploit trust and urgency
  • B. They only target tech experts
  • C. They avoid using emotions

4. What should you do if someone pressures you for confidential information?

  • A. Comply immediately
  • B. Pause and verify the request
  • C. Share it with a coworker

5. Which is a good protective habit?

  • A. Trusting every urgent request
  • B. Staying calm and verifying identity
  • C. Giving out recovery codes

Answer: 1-B, 2-B, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B.

🔑

Password Attacks

Attackers use brute-force, dictionary, or credential stuffing methods to guess passwords.

Explanation

Weak or reused passwords are easy to guess and can be tested across many accounts at once.

Example

Hackers use a stolen password from one website to try logging into your email and banking accounts.

Quick quiz

1. Which password habit is safest?

  • A. Reusing the same password everywhere
  • B. Using a short password with your birth year
  • C. Using a long unique password stored in a password manager

2. Why is password reuse risky?

  • A. It slows down your device
  • B. One breach can expose many accounts
  • C. It makes accounts easier to memorize

3. What does MFA add to password security?

  • A. A second layer of verification
  • B. A longer password
  • C. A password reset tool

4. What is a strong password practice?

  • A. Writing it on a sticky note
  • B. Saving it in a password manager
  • C. Sharing it with a friend

5. What should you do if you think your password was exposed?

  • A. Ignore it
  • B. Change it immediately and enable MFA
  • C. Use it on another site

Answer: 1-C, 2-B, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B.
